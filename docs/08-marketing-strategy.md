# 08 — Marketing Strategy

> Positioning, message hierarchy, content pillars. The marketing job is to convince a tired shop owner to spend 30 minutes with us — not to convince a procurement officer to short-list us.

## Positioning

> Frontline is **the answering desk small businesses actually wanted** — a real, calm voice that picks up the phone, books the appointment, and texts you anything urgent. Built per industry. Priced per minute. Cancellable any month.

Three sharp comparison axes:

| vs. | They are | We are |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic answering services** (LASA, AnswerNet) | Per-minute message-takers | Industry-tuned bookers in your software |
| **Generic AI receptionists** (Numa, Goodcall, the Facebook-ad ones) | Wizard-configured, off-the-shelf voice | Hand-tuned to your shop in 72 hours |
| **Voicemail / Google Voice** | A funnel that drops 76% of callers | A line that actually picks up |

## Message hierarchy

### Top-line
**"We pick up. You stay focused."**

### One-paragraph sell
Frontline runs the front-of-house phone for local businesses — dentists, plumbers, salons, restaurants, clinics, contractors. A calm, real-sounding voice answers every call, books the appointment in your calendar, and hands you a clean note at the end of the day. No missed calls. No phone tag. No "press 1 for English."

### Five-second hook
"Your voice while you're cutting hair."

### Three proof points (in order)
1. **It actually books.** The booking lands in your existing scheduling software (Square, Booksy, Vagaro, Dentrix, Mindbody, Toast, OpenTable). You don't fill out a wizard.
2. **It sounds like a person.** Listen to the dental call on the homepage. We don't ship a generic template — we hand-tune it in a 30-minute call.
3. **It costs what one Saturday no-show costs.** $290/month base + 22¢ per minute over 250 included. No setup fee. Cancel any month.

## Content pillars

Four content pillars; one or two pieces of content per pillar per month.

### Pillar 1 — "Calls we picked up this week"
A weekly redacted-audio clip of an actual call we handled. 30 seconds. Posted on Instagram, TikTok, YouTube Shorts, LinkedIn (for the agency partner audience), and embedded on the homepage's `#sample` anchor.

- **Why it works**: it's the only content type where the prospect can hear, not read, the product. Every objection ("will it sound robotic?") collapses against a 10-second listen.
- **Production rule**: every clip has explicit owner-and-customer permission. We bias toward dental and salon clips for visual diversity (hold music, on-the-fly booking, escalation).

### Pillar 2 — "How a {industry} should answer the phone"
A long-form blog post or 5-minute video per industry. Practical owner advice — what to say in the first three seconds, what to ask before booking, when to escalate. We give it away. Frontline gets mentioned at the end as the "if-you-don't-have-time-for-this" play.

- **Cadence**: one industry per month. Six industries in six months covers the full ICP.
- **Why it works**: ranks for long-tail SEO ("how should a dentist answer the phone"), arms our owner-fans with content they can re-post.

### Pillar 3 — "What we won't do"
A short-form pillar that re-makes our anti-feature manifesto into bite-size posts. "We don't run robocalls. Here's why." "We don't sell call recordings. Here's why."

- **Why it works**: every owner has been burned by a vendor doing the wrong thing. Naming what we won't do builds trust faster than any feature post.
- **Cadence**: one per fortnight. Twitter/X first, then Instagram carousel.

### Pillar 4 — "Behind the desk"
Quarterly look at how we run Frontline. Voice-engineer interviews. The script template. The integrations roadmap. Honest pricing math (how 22¢ per minute breaks down).

- **Why it works**: builds the long-tail trust gradient. Owners share these with their owner friends.
- **Cadence**: one piece per quarter.

## Owner-to-owner amplification

Every customer gets a copy-paste pack to share Frontline in their owner Facebook groups. Three pre-written posts (skeptical, enthusiastic, story-style); they pick one. We never ghost-write — they edit, they post.

This is the marketing function the brand pyramid is engineered for: the owner has to *want* to recommend us. That's why the visual identity is small-business main-street, not enterprise SaaS — when she pastes our link into a Facebook group, the brand has to look like something she'd recommend without embarrassment.

## Brand voice rules (for any marketing copy that ships)

1. **Lead with the picture, not the feature.** "11:48pm on a Tuesday, a clogged drain..." not "After-hours coverage feature."
2. **Use real shops, real numbers.** Tavárez Cuts. Holcomb Plumbing. Greenpoint Family Dental. Lawrence MA. Knoxville TN. Brooklyn. (All used with permission for v1.)
3. **Honest pricing in the open.** Every price point on every marketing page. No "starting at" without the floor; no asterisks.
4. **Anti-features front and center.** Every long piece links to our "what we won't do" list. That's our trust signal.
5. **Plain English.** No "AI-powered customer engagement." Read it aloud — if it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.

## Negative-space marketing rules

- **No paid press releases.** Wrong audience.
- **No influencer partnerships outside trade-credible voices.** Salon-influencer review is fine; tech-Twitter is not.
- **No comparison articles where we name competitors disparagingly.** We name comparisons in product context, not in the negative.
- **No fake testimonials.** Every quote on the site is real, with the owner's permission.

## What "good" looks like at 90 days

- 30 paying shops across all 6 industries.
- 4 weekly Instagram clips with >2,000 plays each.
- Industry blog post #1 ranking top-3 for `"how should a {industry} answer the phone"` in at least two cities.
- Customer NPS ≥ 60 (asked at day 30 via a single email).
- Net revenue retention >100% (customers either expand to Growth or stay flat; <5% churn at 90 days).
